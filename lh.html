    Le Havre

    "Le Havre is a love letter to modernism, evoking, more than any other French city, France’s postwar energy and optimism." - Lonely Planet

    This mini-site is my love letter to Le Havre: an attempt to explain why this is my favourite city in France and one of my favourite places in the world!

     

     

    Is not:

    • Pretentious
    • "Pretty"
    • Overcrowded
    • Posh
    • Expensive
    • A hassle
    • The fine sandy beach of your dreams
    • Very far from Paris
    • Full of Parisians in fancy cars

    Is:

    • Optimistic
    • Remarkable architecture
    • Friendly, open-minded
    • Open
    • Full of long sight-lines
    • An honest beach with normal people
    • A place to watch ships
    • Very walkable
    • Full of industrial/maritime heritage

     

    Imagine...

    Imagine a French town where the people are nice, where you can get good food, where there is a rich cultural offering, where there is the same Normandy light that you get in Deauville but without the Parisians in their Porsches: a town with a beachfront with an impressive sweeping view (culminating in an oil terminal 😂):

    Imagine a town where you can cover everything on foot, but there's a tram as well. Cycle lanes. Big walkways. Pedestrian shopping streets where you can stretch out.

    Imagine a town that was completely devastated in the war, 12,500 buildings: 80% of the city, that had to rebuild itself from scratch, as fast as possible. The visionary architect, Auguste Perret, took a gamble on reinforced concrete, the first ever use of the material for a residential project.

    Imagine a town that had to reimagine its future, so as to arise phoenix-like from the rubble. Where this optimistic, forward-looking, humanist feeling is still palpable today.The whole centre ville was rebuilt in a modular style: a unique architectural feat that led to the town centre being classed as a UNESCO world heritage site in 2005.

    Imagine the first art museum to be built after WWII, containing one of the largest collections of impressionist art in France.

    It's not really this dark and thundery!

    After all, imagine that it was here, in this port, that Monet (who grew up here) imagined the very first impressionist painting:

    Imagine France's second largest sea-port by volume, and the second largest in Europe for total traffic. Where, as a window to the world, they are open to other cultures and ideas, maybe a bit like Liverpool. LH was the starting-point for the transatlantic crossing to New York.

    Imagine a literary town: this is where Sartre was a teacher, where Raymond Queneau was born and raised. A literary walk takes you through various references to LH in classic French literature.

    Imagine a town where architects from around the world come to experiment:

      • The Pont de Normandie, crossing the Seine at LH, was a world first, at 2km long and over 200m high, when it was built in 1995.

      • Even the old power station (now shuttered) is remarkable: in 1967 these were the tallest chimneys in Europe. Today, at 240m, they are still amongst the tallest structures in France. For a while, they were illuminated at night, by a Japanese artist:
